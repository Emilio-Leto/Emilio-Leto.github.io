Spatial Colocation - Noise Complaints in Montreal

This group project used the Spatial Colocation Quotient tool in ArcGIS to test the spatial association between noise complaints and two potential noise sources: sites of road construction and nightlife. Results revealed significant colocation for bars and venues in the downtown core where most nightlife occurs. Complaints significantly colocated with construction sites were more widespread and even clustered in areas with low rates of noise complaints.

The analysis across weekdays and weekends was limited by the shorter operating hours of the 311 lines during the weekends. However, the results may suggest noise is more disruptive during the week, possibly due to greater expectations of tranquility.

Spatial Statistics - Thefts of Vehicles in Vancouver

Hotspot analysis using the Getis-Ord Gi statistic for Map 1 revealed statistically significant clustering for thefts of vehicles in Vancouver. High rates can be observed in the northern and western regions. High rates in central and southern Vancouver may be influenced by the modifiable area unit problem due to the presence of larger dissemination areas there.

Maps 2 and 3 show the distribution of vehicle thefts within the Dunbar-Southlands district of Vancouver in 2009 and 2019, respectively. In addition to the spatial statistics displayed, average nearest neighbour (ANN) analysis was also conducted. Results reveal a southward shift in thefts from 2009 to 2019, as well as less clustering in 2019 (ANN ratio of 1.27) compared to 2009 (ANN ratio of 1.32).

Urban Heat Islands - Using Citizen Data in Montreal

This map utilized citizen sensor data to analyze Montreal’s heat islands on June 29th 2019. The map shows cooler temperatures in the west of the island compared to the east, possibly due to its lower building density. Furthermore, the presence of large open spaces such as the Pierre Elliott Trudeau Airport in the West Island may provide a cooling effect. Similarly, cool pockets are observed in downtown Montreal over large parks including Mount Royal and La Fontaine in the Plateau (PM).

Rivers also provide a cooling effect due to water’s high heat capacity and the presence of convection currents along riverbanks. As the Saint Lawrence is widest in the west and has a total greater perimeter compared to the east, this exacerbates the cooling effect in the west.
